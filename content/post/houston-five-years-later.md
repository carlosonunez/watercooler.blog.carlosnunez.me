Our five-year anniversary of living in Houston passed a few days ago. This felt
like the perfect excuse to finally publish this New Yorker's thoughts on living in Houston ---
and Dallas, and, well, Texas in general. I finally had some downtime to kill
during my road trip from the Northeast, so, here it is.

Please! Hold your applause. I know all four of you have been waiting a while for
this to drop; sorry for the delay.

They're good thoughts too! In a nutshell, if you can put aside the vagaries of
living atop a gigantic swamp and the hot, moist weather it bestows for half of
the year, **there is no better bang for your buck than living in Houston, bar
none.**

(Here's why you should read this if you're not one of those lucky four --- okay,
maybe three:

- I was born and raised in NYC and lived there for a significant part of my
  adult life.

- I also lived in Dallas for several years before moving to the 713 and spent
  significant time throughout the city, and I've spent a lot of time in Austin,
  the city that most think of most immediately when they think of Texas.

- As well, I am an extremely experienced traveler who has visited and/or spent
  significant time in every major city in the continental United States and many
  of the smaller ones as well. (I'm working on Hawai'i and Alaska; stay tuned!)

- Finally, I lived in the Houston suburbs as well as the "Inner Loop" for long
  enough to understand the advantages and disadvantages of both.

There aren't many soapboxes for me to stand on. Living in Houston is one of
them!)

The "living atop a gigantic swamp" is important, so let's get the bad stuff that
you're expecting (and probably already know about) out of the way.

# The Cons

## Weather/Geography

{{< figure src="/images/houston/skyline.png" >}}

_Not shown: several layers of hot, wet, and murky_

A bit of history first.

Houston is a bit of a historical accident. In the late 1800s,
Galveston, a beach town about 45 minutes south of the famous skyline shown
above, was the economic engine keeping Texas afloat.  Its proximity to the Gulf
provided the means for the Port of Galveston to become the second busiest port
in America behind New York/New Jersey. At the time, despite a slowly-growing
downtown, Houston was, by comparison, a mud pit merchants had to cross to get to
Galveston.

{{< figure src="/images/houston/galveston.png" >}}

_Imagine sea-side skyscrapers and a bustling business crowd here_

It is often assumed that the Great Flood of 1900 was what forced this grand mud
pit to become Texas's next great economic powerhouse, and it _did_ significantly
and inexorably damage Galveston's infrastructure...but that isn't the whole
story.

Instead, it was the happenstance discovery of "black gold", first in Beaumont,
then in Humble (the "H" is silent), that changed Houston's --- and Galveston's
--- futures. With the discovery of one of the largest reserves of oil in the
world at the time (it took mostly-untrained engineers 12 days to stop the
massive overflow of oil gushing from Humble's Spindletop gusher) came the need
to refine, store and ship it.

These events stoked a newly-found interest in the intersection of the Buffalo
and White Oak Bayous in Deer Park/Galena. While it wasn't _directly_ on the
Gulf, it did provide a wide enough pathway into it, and the additional distance
provided good-enough protection from devastating hurricanes and tropical storms.

_Good-enough._

<hr />

Another tidbit of Houston's history that isn't often talked about (I'm getting
there, I promise) is its importance to rice farming in Texas.

While rice was historically imported from the East, The Gulf Coast has long been
an key asset to America's domestic rice production (and, along with cotton, was
one of two crops that the South used to justify slavery, and the Civil War that
followed to end the practice). What is now the greater Houston metro has been
central to that; by 1903, this area was responsible for 99% of America's rice
production capabilities.[^5]

A quick browse to `usarice.com` tells us that rice is "a semi-aquatic plant that
requires consistent irrigation all season to grow.  Heavy clay and silt loam
soils that are often ill-suited to other crops retain water very well, making
them perfect for rice."[^4]

<hr />

This brings us, finally, to Houston's biggest thorn: its climate.

Wikipedia tells us that Houston was built atop a
"flat, marshy area" [^0] with a "humid subtropical" climate [^1]. My five years
here agrees with these assessments. These beget benefits and disadvantages. Since
we're talking about the negatives of living here, I'll cover the disadvantages
first.

{{< figure src="/images/houston/weather.png" >}}

_A typical daily forecast during the "sticky" season._

Houston is hot and _sticky_ for a non-trivial part of the year; usually July to
September, though I've seen this period be shorter and longer in my time here.
There are several days where you _will_ sweat when you leave your house, no
matter the time of day (even at night!). Waking up at 4am will _not_ proivde
refuge during this _moist_ time of the year.

Then there's the rain. Most folks think of the Pacific Northwest or, well, NYC
when they think of rainy climates. None of them were rice growing
empires, however. Houston was. Instead of the constant, mountaineous and
comfortable mild drizzles of Seattle and Portland, Houston brings these
impressive if not downright terrifying onslaughts of water from the sky that
you think you can predict but often can't. Sunny in one moment, soaking in the
next is normal for Houston. Sometimes, you'll even be able to see rain bands
floating atop distant Houston cities while driving down I-10, which is always a
cool sight.

Houston is also flat. Near zero elevation as far as the eye can see. This +
clay-like soil + massive body of water a throwing distance away = floods. Lots
of floods. Houston loves to flood. It's exceptional at it! Most of Houston is
either in a FEMA flood zone or hasn't been updated by FEMA yet to enter one. The
city has invested a lot of engineering time working around this problem, like
Memorial Parkway that, on most days, looks and works like a normal parkway in
the middle of downtown but converts into a massive and absolutely shocking storm
channel that basically becomes a WHOLE NEW ASS RIVER in record time.

{{< figure src="/images/houston/memorial.png" >}}

_This never stops being cool_

Besides the minor threat of being washed away sometimes, the only real
disadvantage of being in a flood zone is having to purchase flood insurance from
FEMA every year (home insurance DOES NOT cover floods!!!!!). Fortunately, it's
"only" about $1400/year. The floods clear about as quickly as they build, so
it's a minor inconvenience on most days.

Finally, Houston is flat, so Houston lacks a lot of the more desirable nature
you'll find the PNW, Appalachia or the Catskills. More specifically, all of the
nature trails and state parks --- we only have two --- are flat and
_exceptionally hot_ in the open areas. Houston is also, but not entirely,
famously unwalkable [^6], so walking to places can be a real emergency if you
don't carry enough hydration in the summer months.

## Public Transit/Driving

Houston doesn't have trains to their airports.

Most of the buses in Houston METRO run every 30 minutes, with _way more than I'd
like_ running every _60_ minutes.

**All** of the Houston suburbs "have" buses, but in the "we're forced to provide
this" sense, so, basically, they don't have _any_ viable public transit options
in a practical sesnse.

Thus, you'll likely be married to your car while living here. Which means you'll
be exposed to Houston's driving culture, which can be summed up with this:
"pitch the insanity of Mad Max and the comedy of Mario Kart into a large pot, add some
inadequate infrastructure, stir, and serve. Best enjoyed with good true crime
podcasts or audiobooks and Nepalese Monk-levels of patience."

Speeding is table stakes here. Even the cops speed. Driving the speed limit
makes for fast enemies --- I know, since I do it! Tailgating, cutoffs,
right-lane exits from the left lane, red-light running, drunk driving and
incredible levels of phone addiction are the norm here. A few days on Houston
roads is enough to see how the city earned its spot as one of the deadliest
driving cities in America.[^7] It's the first place I lived where I felt like I
needed GoPros on my helmet and bike while cycling in the suburbs (and they STILL
weren't enough!) --- and I commuted by bike in NYC for years!

Despite all of that, my car mostly gathers dust during the week.

There are ways around being chained to your car, enough to consider them an
**advantage** of living here, so I'll save them for the "pros" section. 

# The Pros

That's it for my cons. There are a LOT of pros to living in "The H" --- at least
a blog post's worth.

I love living here, and I'm _beyond_ excited to finally share these with you. I
hope you finish this feeling even a tenth of the excitement I felt while typing
this.

## Weather/Climate

{{< figure src="/images/houston/winter.png" >}}

_The flipside of the forecast you saw before. A typical Houston day in December.
Merry Christmas!_

**Compared to Austin**: Austin is less moist, but just as hot. More "dry" heat,
but not dry like Phoenix or Albuquerque. They get an _ever so slightly_ more
aggressive winter.

**Compared to Dallas**: Dallas is milder, but they get a harsher winter.
Definitely not Minnesota level, but they're more likely to get snowed in for the
one or two days Texas sees snow. Also, tornados. Hope you like bathrooms! [^9]

**Compared to NYC**: Oh boy. You couldn't pay me enough to go back to NYC weather.
December through April is cold, dark and snowy (or a gross kind of slushy if you
live in the city. May through September is moist and hot, though not as much as
Houston is. Yep, you'll still deal with mosquitos and flies...and that spotted
lanternfly thing you're legally supposed to squish on contact. Garbage is also
collected streetside in NYC. Eight million people == LOTS of trash. Thus, much
of NYC smells like a rotting dumpster fire during these months, except for the
Chinatowns, which have the privilege of smelling like rotting fish. October
through December is mostly fine until you see the sunset times roll back to 4pm
quicker than you anticipate and get sad again.

<hr />

Houston is hot; you know this already. As a result, I wear shorts and sandals
90% of the year.

Putting up Christmas in my Pace Breakers and custom huaraches NEVER gets old: a
lifetime of doing so in sub-20/30 deg F (about -7/-1 deg C) weather with snow on
the ground will do that.

Indeed, you'll sweat at 4am and sweat _faster_ at 12pm during the sticky season.
I'm actually a defected human being and _enjoy_ this. However, even if I didn't,
this is still a very solid tradeoff for being able to enjoy the same weather
that Chicago and Seattle receive for three summer months _for half of the year_.
I've enjoyed many "San Diego" weather experiences here, usually between November
and May, sometimes even June. "I'll sit on the patio" type weather. It's great.

What's also great is a piece of history I forgot to mention in the history
section: Carrier invented the first commercial air conditioner for the JP Morgan
building here in Houston. **Houston is the most air-conditioned city in the world.** 

This combined with our cheap electricity rates means that you'll spend much of
your existence in crisp, 72 deg F (22 deg C) comfort. Unlike much of the North,
_EVERYWHERE_ is air-conditioned here. **_EVERYWHERE!_** An annoying side effect
of this is the "freezer effect" wherein you'll feel like you're walking into a
freezer after spending even a few minutes walking in the Houston heat.

There are very few places that do not have central air; in fact, there is
actually legislation up for vote [^8] that mandates landlords to install air
conditioning in exactly these rental units; it'll probably have passed by the
time you read this.

{{< figure src="/images/houston/snow.png" >}}

_Many Houston kids had the time of their lives in this buildup. It was gone the
next day._

We also have a cold season for snow birds to enjoy, though it is markedly milder
than the Northeast and even somewhat milder than the winters my wife and I
experienced in Dallas. First, it, pretty consistently, only lasts about a few
weeks spread out over the course of two months. Second, it usually never gets
colder than 20 degrees, almost always at night. Third, **_it almost never snows
here_**, and when it does, the gods treat it as a bug and disappear it the next
day.

Then there's the rain. Indeed, buckets of rain are dumped atop of the Houston
MSA every year. As bad as the flooding can get, on average, this is actually a
great thing for several reasons:

1. Have you ever tried running in the rain? IT'S A BLAST!

2. Your plants and dog will love it!

3. I haven't experienced a drought since moving here. In fact, between this, our
   water treatment systems and being home to [one of the largest aquafers in the
   United
   States](https://www.twdb.texas.gov/groundwater/aquifer/majors/gulf-coast.asp),
   water is quite plentiful here! People are pretty wreckless with water as a
   result. Sprinklers while it's raining --- that sort of thing. This also means
   _very very cheap water_ compared against the national average. Los Angelinos
   hate us for this!

Speaking of water: Houston is also home to [The
Cistern](https://buffalobayou.org/location/the-cistern/), an former drinking
reservoir turned art exhibit along the Buffalo Bayou. Seeing acapella
performances here has been a real treat and is unlike anything I've seen
anywhere. I always recommend that folks check this out during their time here.

{{< figure src="/images/houston/cistern.png" >}}

_Voices propagate beautifully within this historic Houston artifact._

## Public Transportation

**Compared to Austin**: Houston has the edge here IMO. Houston's
bus to the airport is at this time of writing MUCH nicer than the 20 to
Bergstrom. Austin's only 15-mins or better buses are part of their Bus Rapid
Transit network, though you can get from South to North Austin with them fairly
easily. Republic Square is fucking terrifying despite being right across the
street from Hotel ZaZa, my favorite hotel in Austin behind Heywood Hotel on East
Cesar Chavez. CapMetro has a single heavy rail train --- the Red Line --- that
takes you to Domain and, mysteriously, Downtown Leander, but it stops running at
6pm on weekdays and doesn't run on Sundays.

**Compared to Dallas**: Draw. Dallas has DART rail, despite Tarrant and Colin
Counties wishing for it to go away. Like most subway/elevated rail networks,
DART centralizes around downtown. Unfortunately, most of the action in Dallas
isn't there! This means that you're often at the behest of the DART bus network,
the heel of the DART network. Most DART buses run hourly. Some run twice an
hour. An even smaller handful --- mostly within Dallas proper --- run every 15
minutes. It takes an hour _at best_ to get from Downtown Plano to Legacy West,
i.e. where the jobs and shops are. This is a 20 minute drive on local roads. If
you live outside of Dallas proper, Carrolton or Denton, you're SOL. No buses for
you!

**Compared to NYC**: LOL. No comparison. MTA NYC fuckin' rules. Surprisingly,
living in NYC _did_ get easier once we got a car. Shopping at Trader Joe's in Staten
Island was a MUCH nicer experience than shopping at Trader Joe's on Court
Street. That said, HEB was a life-changing experience; absolutely nothing
compares. Regardless, NYC absolutely has the upper hand here.

<hr />

I mentioned previously that many of the buses in the Houston METRO system run
every 30 minutes, with more than a handful of them running every hour.

Houston also has a VERY extensive bus network.

{{< figure src="/images/houston/bus.png" >}}

_VERY extensive! No cash required, though virtual METRO cards give you a free
ride every 10 rides._

You _can_ get from East Houston to West Houston by way of twice-an-hour buses.
It's inconvenient, but definitely doable.

If you live within the "Inner Loop" --- the collection of neighborhoods within
the I-610/US-59 (**_not_** the I-69) quadrangle...and EaDo --- you can get
_pretty much anywhere_ by bus _and light rail_ alone.

Yes! Houston _has_ a light rail! That network is much, much smaller, but its
trains will get you from downtown to NRG Stadium or
EaDo roughly every six minutes. METRO even optioned several new trains from
Siemens to add to the network!

Houston also has several buses in the "Frequent Network." These run every 15
minutes _or sooner_. In the case of the 82 WESTHEIMER, the 85
ANTOINE/WASHINGTON  or the 65 BISSONNET, _or sooner_ is usually the case. Thanks
to Houston's time as a host city for the 2026 World Cup, this is one of few bus
networks within the US that runs past midnight as well. This is very useful for
folks working --- or partying --- the night shift.

Then, there's this:

{{< figure src="/images/houston/waymo.png" >}}

Waymo has been operating in Houston since April 2026. It covers the entire inner
loop and a good chunk of the path to NRG as well. Unlike Austin, you can book
Waymos through the Waymo app, a superior experience to Uber, in my opinion.

It is difficult to overstate how EXTREMELY FUCKING AWESOME it is to hail a
rideshare that (a) is on time, (b) doesn't require tipping (a big statement
coming from this very-pro-tipping individual), (c) doesn't require small talk,
and (d) follows all traffic laws and drives more safely than most drivers,
including me.

While it's not _really_ public transit --- I'd actually prefer 15-minute-max
bus headways and real commuter rail to the suburbs --- it solidly fills the gap
while Houston builds their network up. (The now-very-demolished Northwest Mall
on Washington Ave will, politics notwithstanding, become one of three stations
for the high speed network coming online in 2032-ish.)

Like I said earlier, my car mostly gathers dust during the week. We were
lucky enough to move to a house near the 26 LONG POINT / CAVALCADE line. This
line is on the frequent network and Long Point Avenue / 20th Street has OH MY
GOD SO MANY amazing establishments along it, a big plus in and of itself. It
also connects with the 700 RED LINE light rail at Fulton Street, a straight shot
downtown that drops me off right in front of work AND connects to the 82
WESTHEIMER and 85 ANTOINE/WASHINGTON buses. All of this along with my area being
_extremely_ walkable culminates to a greatly reduced need for my car. At least
until I need to huff it to the 'burbs, which brings me to my closing point for
this section:

_Houston within the Inner Loop is a COMPLETELY DIFFERENT WORLD from Houston
outside of the Inner Loop._

This isn't necessary a bad thing depending on what you're looking for, as I'll
explain soon.
