or "being an SE as a wannabe hacker that still kind of wants to hack but enjoys
the travel and customer life", but that's a long-ass post title and the greek
mythology reference will make sense later.

---

i've been cautiously cosplaying as a nerdy sales engineer/ex-wannabe-hacker
thing for about three years now. it's been interesting just as much as it's been
confusing.

the canonical books on Sales Engineering™[^0] describe sales engineering pretty
succinctly: "to help your sales counterparts advance and close sales by
obtaining the technical win." my experiences to date agree with this definition.

in practice, it's a bit of a weird role to describe to people at parties.
finding a clever analogy to use here was a bit of a challenge. a
clever redditor summarizes our job as "geek squad for AEs," which is hilarious
and highly accurate, so that's what i'm going with.

it's an awesome career to be in once you're "done" being an full-time
professional engineer. you fly places ("fly" mostly being back-to-back video
calls these days; thanks, COVID!) to talk tech to technical people, write some
screeds in salesforce, enjoy doing essentially-whatever [^2] during
downtime and get a very comfortable check every two weeks or so. opaque and
oft-politcial bonus programs are eschewed for commission, whose
much-too-wordy-most-of-the-time terms and conditions usually reduce to "sell more; earn
more". agile is spoken about in the third-person and no ae that you're
partnered with will understand you when you tell them about "story points."

you'll never play planning poker again.

heck, you can probably get away with never opening a terminal again for some SE
roles.[^1]

what's not to love? it's an excellent alternative to grinding up the ladder to
principal SWE/SRE. you won't get those sky-high RSU allocations, but you also
won't be forced to move to the bay or new york, since salespeople are always on
the road, making having them on-site kind-of pointless. [^3]

well, theoretically.

what if you're not "done?"

---

{{< figure src="/images/cerberus/just-one-more-lane-bro.jpg" >}}

i often say that i'm a "broken man."

i have always loved commuting. 45-minute jaunts on the subway have been just as
enjoyable as hour-long bouts in traffic.  needless to say, taking up a job in
2016 that had me on a plane twice a week, every week was one of the best things
to happen to me. the thrill of navigating airports was the missing piece in a
puzzle i didn't know i was solving. nothing gets me going more than a rolling
delay [^4] or a mid-route diversion.

i'm in a weird place in my career (well, _was_ while i wrote this). my career
aspirations is basically a terrifying cerberus. like the ones on nasty speed
metal vinyl covers that signal that you're probably in for some dementedly
good shit.

---

{{< figure src="/images/cerberus/metal-af.jpg" >}}

one of the dog heads loves being an SE. back-to-back meetings suck, but staff
engineers deal with that anyway _before_ reviewing PRs. they'll probably need to
play stories too given how convincing code by Claude Code is these days, but
that's a rant for another watercooler screed. talking about tech for a living is
fun, and having direct, unquestionable influence in how money comes in is really
cool. i can point to meetings i've had that turned into revenue. this was
basically impossible as a consultant, though i'm really proud of what i've
accomplished as one.

another dog head desperately wants to go back to engineering full-time. i still
love writing code. that i can't make time to code more is a massive source of
frustration i'm trying to come to grips with.[^7] letting this dog head win would
solve that. the life of picking stories, shipping code, reviewing PRs and
gesticulating loudly while estimating only to land up throwing out a `5` is
irresistably attractive some days. definitely on days where my calendar looks
like a completed jenga puzzle others played on my behalf.

another dog head barks violently at the "just go back to SRE, bro" dog head
whenever it starts talking.

"UNPREDICTABLE BONUS STRUCTURES!"

"UMM, LAYOFFS!" [^5]

"YOU'RE ABOUT 27 MONTHS AWAY FROM BEING REPLACED BY A VSCODE EXTENSION!"

"DO YOU REALLY WANT TO LIVE IN NEW YORK AGAIN? WAIT, HANG ON, **YOU ACTUALLY FUCKING
DON'T!**"

Stuff like that.

then there's the dog head that just wants a chill job wher i can travel every
week again doing fun, computery shit. "THAT WORLD IS GONE! GET OVER IT!," the
angry, barking dog often rages.

finally, there's the stoner-looking dog head that keeps muttering "but what if
we, like, you know, just, stopped working?" i often ignore them, since i
actually like working most of the time, but they're most-convincing when i wake
up at 07:30 when I naturally want to wake up closer to 10:00.[^6]

in case you're wondering, it is DEFINITELY very loud in my brain during most
working weeks.  it's on my mind a lot.

---

nonetheless, i stand by my stance from the beginning of this article. SE is an awesome
career for engineers who want something just a little different. it's basically
a shoe-in for ex-consultants; it's basically that job plus commission and an
all-expenses-paid week somewhere fancy if you're lucky minus the usual
bullshit that comes with consulting.[^8]

but. if you're a nerd that identifies more with the
[compiling xkcd comic](https://xkcd.com/303/) than the wonderful nuances of MEDDPICC,
you'll probably progenate a very loud, and very annoying, cerberus of your own.

there are more nerd-heavy SE roles out there, but at the end of the day, getting
that technical win is your job, and your time slinging code will be sacrificed
in service of that.

more experienced SEs i've spoken to about this have told me that this contention
will eventually quiet down. time will tell, i suppose.
